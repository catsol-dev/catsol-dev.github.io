The New Deal

By Randy Coyle and Charlie Hepperlen

Background

During the 1930s, the USA's economy was doing very horribly. The great depression had a horrible effect all across the world, causing people to lose everything they had, businesses to fail, and other various bad things. As a means to recover, president Franklin Delano Roosevelt put together a large series of reforms over the early-mid 30s.

Goals

There were many goals in mind with the New Deal.
  • Reduce mass unemployment common amongst American workers.
  • Generally restore the economy as much as physically possible.
  • Reduce suffering of the American people.
  • Stabilize the economy.
  • Improve the lives of working Americans' labor rights.
  • Guarantee the lives of those who cannot provide for themselves.

Methodology

Many different programs and agencies were put in place for the New Deal. Prohibition was completely ended. The president himself, in what would end up known as the first fireside chat, asked Americans to put their savings into the banks, allowing them to reopen. The National Industrial Recovery Act massively advanced workers' rights, guaranteeing the right to unionize and use said union. The Tennessee Valley Authority Act was signed, giving the ability to the federal government to build dams and subsequently provide hydroelectric power to those around the Tennessee River. An artificial food scarcity was created in order to boost the agricultural industry, with incentives for farmers who complied. The Public Works Administration was created. Another series of efforts was made later, establishing the Works Progress Administration, providing many jobs to those in need. The Wagner Act, officially the National Labor Relations Act, ensured that union elections and the treatment of workers was fair. The Social Security Act was signed, which through many different means created a system that could provide for those who needed it, and allow people to get back into things. Many of the programs created still exist to this day, being greatly beneficial to many Americans.

Opposition

While agreed by most today to be a good thing, the New Deal faced much opposition in its time. There was a conservative majority on the Supreme Court, which often obstructed programs. As a solution, Roosevelt decided to try and pack the court with justices who would agree with his plans. While the conservative justices eventually voted in favor of the plan, the attempts did large amounts of damage to the reputation of the administration, and paved the way for future opposition until WW2.

Results

Ultimately, the New Deal did do a great deal to combat the great depression, at least until WW2 happened. It did not end the great depression, but it absolutely alleviated suffering. In terms of employment rate, this graph from the Bureau of Labor Statistics should say a great deal on the help provided.
While the New Deal did not alleviate nearly as much as the efforts for WW2, by no means was it not an impressive feat. On top of the reduced unemployment, jobs got better to work in. By the end of the decade, millions of workers had organized and formed unions. Many advances were made that benefited the average American, and likely helped even into WW2. In terms of combating the great depression, the New Deal did achieve its goals, and many remnants of it still exist to this day, in spite of the adversity it faced from a variety of opponents. Additionally, many from the business world were heavily opposed to the New Deal for fairly obvious reasons.
